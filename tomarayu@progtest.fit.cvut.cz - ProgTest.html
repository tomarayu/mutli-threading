The task is to develop a class which quickly calculates the price of steel plates.

Assume a company that produces welded steel plates. The company accepts customer requests with a specification of steel plates the customer demands. The specification includes dimensions and material of the plates. The problem is to calculate the price of such steel plates and return the filled requests back to the customer.

The steel plates are always of a rectangular (or square) shape. The dimensions are provided as two positive integers: the width and height. Next, there is the material specification (e.g., thickness, steel quality, ...), these properties are given by another integer - the material identifier materialID. The requested size may either be directly available from the producer or be welded from two or more smaller steel plates. The welding adds another quality parameter - the strength. We assume that the customer specifies the required welding strength as a parameter in their request, the strength is given as a floating point number. For the sake of simplicity, let the same number be the unit cost of the welding (the cost of welding of length 1 unit). Our company has the equipment to weld the steel plates; however, it does not have any tool to cut them. Therefore, the dimension of the welded steel plate must exactly match the dimension that is requested by the customer.

Our company buys steel plates from steel mills (producers). Each producer produces steel plates in different dimensions and for different cost. Therefore, the producers must be contacted, and their price lists must be analyzed to correctly calculate the price of the steel plates the customer requires. The producers provide their price lists for the given material; the material is identified by the materialID. A price list consists of 0 to N entries (0 entries means that the producer does not sell any steel plates of the given material). Each entry in the price list provides the size of the steel plate and its cost. Our company cooperates with one or more producers; the same material may be offered by one or more producers. Each producer has its price list, the dimensions and the prices may vary in price lists from different producers. Since the material is the same, we are free to combine steel sheets bought from different producers and decrease the final cost.

We know the dimensions and prices of the available steel sheets once the price lists are collected. Moreover, the cost of welding is known from the customer request, thus the cost of the requested steel sheets may be calculated. The general case is somewhat technical; however, our welding technology limits the possible combinations which in turn simplifies the computation. Our welding machine can only weld two steel sheets along equally long edges (see figure below). This simplification leads to an algorithm with cubic time complexity. Anyway, cubic algorithms tend to be rather slow, thus we use multiple threads to increase the computation speed.

Your primary task is to design and implement classes that efficiently distribute the computation workload to multiple threads. You do not have to develop and implement the computation algorithm itself - there is a class CProgtestSolver available in the testing environment. The class may be used to calculate the cost of a steel sheet from the parameters. A correct solution that is based on this class will pass all mandatory and optional tests. On the other hand, the class cannot be used in the bonus tests. First, the class intentionally provides invalid results in the bonus tests. Second, a different interface would be better suited for the bonus tests.

Your solution needs to implement CWeldingCompany class, the implemented class must include the required interface below. Next, you may add further support classes and functions to your solution. CWeldingCompany class uses certain support classes that are implemented in the testing environment. A simplified implementation of these classes is provided in the attached archive. These are classes CProd, CPriceList, COrder, COrderList, CProducer, and CCustomer. The implementation of the above six classes is fixed, you are not allowed to modify them in any way.

The program will use several threads:

  • the main thread creates an instance of CWeldingCompany, fills in customers and producers, and starts the computation by invoking CWeldingCompany::start. The start method creates your threads, in particular:
  • each customer will need a service thread. The customer threads are supposed to wait for requests from the customers and to pass these requests to the worker threads. The customer threads are not designed to do any time-intensive computation. The number of customer support threads is determined by the number of registered customers. Your implementation is responsible for the customer threads; it shall start them and wait for their completion,
  • worker threads. The worker threads are designated to do all the expensive computation. Therefore, the number of worker threads may be controlled by a parameter passed to the start method. Your implementation is responsible for these threads, i.e., it shall start and terminate them,
  • finally, there may exist other threads in the program. The testing environment creates threads for producers; the producer threads are used to deliver price lists to your CWeldingCompany::addPriceList method asynchronously. Like the customer threads, these threads are used just to pass some data and are not intended to do any expensive computations. The producer threads are created and terminated by the testing environment.

Once the threads are created in CWeldingCompany::start, the main thread returns to the caller and does whatever it needs to. Finally, the main thread invokes CWeldingCompany::stop. Your implementation waits until all customers requests are read, processed, and returned. When the last customer request is finished, the implementation stops all threads it created (i.e., it stops and joins worker threads and customer threads). Then it returns from CWeldingCompany::stop to its caller.

Class CProgtestSolver is an abstract class that provides an interface to compute the input problems. The actual implementation is hidden in a subclass, the subclass in not directly accessible. Either way, the solver is avaialable in the testing environment and in the attached library. The instances of the solver are created by factory function createProgtestSolver().

Class CProgtestSolver may be used in both mandatory and optional tests. The implementation, however, does not work in bons tests. When attempted to create in the bonus test, the instance retuns invalid results, freezes, crashes, or does not work in some other creative way. If you plan to pass the bonus tests, you have to implement the solver on your own.

The interface of CProgtestSolver is a programmer challenge. If you decide to use CProgtestSolver, you may save some time on the implementation of the algorithm, however, the interface strikes back on the synchronization. First, the instance of CProgtestSolver is designed to solve a batch of problems. The problems to solve are added one-by-one by a call to method addProblem. Each CProgtestSolver has a certain capacity, the capacity limits the maximum number od problem instances you are allowed to add. A call to method addProblem fails once the capacity is reached. The status of the solver instance may be checked by a call to method hasFreeCapacity. The computation is started when you decide to do so. Technically, you may decide to start the computation at any moment, even with half-empty solver instance, or even with an instance that contains just one problem to solve. Nevertheless, the solver capacity is a scarce resource. The testing environment limits the total number of problems it is willing to solve, this number is split into the created solver instances. The total limit is set to provide enough solving capacity for all problems passed in the test, yet the limit is quite tight. If your implementation does not use the full capacity of the solvers, the unused capacity is lost. This way, you may run out of solvers (the testing environment creates solvers with zero capacity once the limit is exhausted). Therefore, it is reasonable to fully allocate the capacity of all CProgtestSolver instances.

The computation may start when all problems are added to the CProgtestSolver instance. There is method solve() to trigger the computation. This is where the second challenge of CProgtestSolver comes. The method solve must be called by one or more worker threads. Each CProgtestSolver instance defines its required number of threads, the number may be determined by a call to totalThreads(). For instance, CProgtestSolver may require 3 worker threads to call solve() method. When the first two threads call solve(), the instance does nothing and the threads are blocked in the call. When the third thread calls solve(), the computation is started and all three threads participate in the computation of the stored problems. The threads subsequently finish the computation and return from solve(). When the last thread returns from solve(), the stored problems are solved.

The implementation does not care about the price list in its parameter; it silently assumes that the price list is consistent. If the price list is inconsistent, the function may provide arbitrary results. A price list is inconsistent, if it lists the same steel sheet several times with different prices. For example: 

W  H  Cost
1  1  10
1  1  20
The sheets may be rotated when assembled into the final product, thus the following is also a "duplicate": 
W  H  Cost
5  7  10
7  5  20
The caller of the solver is responsible for the price list, i.e., it must remove the duplicates.

Hints:
  • You do not have to implement the price calculation algorithm itself. Use CProgtestSolver instead. Focus on the threads and synchronization. Once your program works with CProgtestSolver, you may start with your own implementation of the solver.
  • To be able to use more CPU cores, process several customer requests in parallel. Use customer service threads and call waitForDemand methods from all customers. Do not calculate the price in the customer service threads; let worker threads do the job. When prices are calculated by the worker threads, the requests must be passed back to the respective customers. There is no need to preserve the relative order of the submitted requests. A worker thread that finished the calculation of the price may pass the completed request back to the customer. You have to calculate prices for several customer requests simultaneously. That is the basic idea to use the power of the available CPUs.
  • The instances of CWeldingCompany are created repeatedly for various inputs. Don't rely on global variable initialization. The global variable will have different values in the second, third, and further tests. An alternative is to initialize global variables always in constructor or start method. Not to use global variables is even better.
  • Don't use mutexes and conditional variables initialized by PTHREAD_MUTEX_INITIALIZER. There are the same reasons as in the paragraph above. Use pthread_mutex_init() instead. Or use C++11 API.
  • The instances of COrderList are allocated by the testing environment when smart pointers are initialized. They are deallocated automatically when all references are destroyed. Don't free those instances; it is sufficient to forget all copies of the smart pointers. But, on the other hand, your program has to free all resources it allocates.
  • The requests need to be solved continuously (read, processed, and passed back to customers). Do not attempt to read all requests into some data structures and then start the processing itself. The technique won't work - you will end up in a deadlock when there are two or more threads. Moreover, you should simultaneously serve requests from all customers. If you try to serve problems from customer A, then B, etc., you will cause a deadlock too.
  • Method CCustomer::completed is reentrant. There is no need to serialize access to the method, do not lock the call with a mutex. Any number of worker threads may call the method simultaneously.
  • Don't use exit, pthread_exit or similar calls in stop or in any other method. If stop method does not return back to its caller, your program will be evaluated as wrong.
  • Use sample data in the attached files. There you can find an example of API calls, several test data sets, and the corresponding results.
  • The test environment uses STL. Be careful as the same STL container must not be accessed from multiple threads concurrently. You can find more information about STL parallel access in C++ reference - thread safety.
  • Test environment has a limited amount of memory. There is no explicit limit, but the virtual machine, where tests are run has RAM size limited to 4GB. Your program is guaranteed at least 500MB of memory (i.e., data segment + stack + heap). The rest of the physical RAM is used by OS, and other processes.
  • If you choose to solve all bonus tasks, be careful to use proper granularity of parallelism. The input problem must be divided into several subproblems to pass the bonus tests. On the other hand, if there are too many small problems, context switches induce a high overhead. The reference solution limits the maximum number of problems concurrently solved by the worker threads to avoid this overhead.
What do the particular tests mean:
Test algoritmu (sekvencni) [Algorithm test]
The test environment calls methods CWeldingCompany::usingProgtestSolver() to determine the solver you use. If you implement your own solver, the tester calls CWeldingCompany::seqSolve() for various inputs and checks the computed results. The purpose of the test is to check your algorithm. No instance of CWeldingCompany is created, no start method is called. You can check whether your implementation is fast enough with this test. The test data are randomly generated.
Základní test [Basic test]
The test environment creates an instance of CWeldingCompany for different number of worker threads (W=xxx), producers (P=xxx) and customers (C=xxx).
Test zahlcení [Flood test]
The test environment generates a vast amount of requests and it checks whether your implementation can handle it. If you do not control the number of started requests, you will probably exhaust memory limit.
Test zrychleni vypoctu [Speedup test]
The test environment runs your implementation for the same input data with a various number of worker threads. The test measures the time required for the computation (real and CPU times). As the number of worker threads increases, the real time should decrease, and CPU time can slightly increase (the number of worker threads is below the number of physical CPU cores). If the real time does not decrease or does not decrease enough, the test is failed. For example, the real time shall drop to 0.5 of the sequential time if two worker threads are used. In reality, the speedup will not be 2. Therefore, there is some tolerance in the comparison.
Busy waiting - pomali zakaznici [Busy waiting - slow customers]
There is a sleep call inserted into the waitForDemand calls (e.g. 100 ms sleep). Worker threads then do not have anything to do. If worker threads are not synchronized/blocked properly, CPU time increases and the test fails.
Busy waiting - pomali producenti [Busy waiting - slow producers]
There is a significant delay before producers return the price lists. If the worker threads are not synchronized/blocked properly, CPU time is increased, and the test fails.
Busy waiting - pomale akceptace [Busy waiting - slow accepts]
There are delays inserted in CCustomer::completed methods. If customer service threads are not synchronized/blocked, properly, CPU time increases and the test fails.
Test rozlozeni zateze 1 [Load balance test 1]
The test environment tries, whether a single customer request engages more than one thread. There is just one customer request, it consists of several steel sheets, i.e., COrderList::m_List size is more than one. If you want to pass this test, your program needs to split AOrderList into several subtasks running in parallel. This test is a bonus test.
Test rozlozeni zateze 2 [Load balance test 2]
The test environment tries, whether single customer request engages more than one thread. There is just one customer request which consists of just one huge steel sheet, i.e., COrderList::m_List size is exactly one. If you want to pass this test, your program needs to split the calculation of the price into several subtasks running in parallel. This test is a bonus test.
